# How to choose food supplements: a practical guide without empty promises

Food supplements (dietary supplements) are everywhere: pharmacies, online shops, ads, podcasts and influencer tips. That makes it hard to separate a useful purchase from noise. This guide is for people who want to shop thoughtfully — not from fear and not from miracle claims. The goal is not to “sell everything”, but to show how to read a label, when a supplement can support a diet, and when it is smarter to fix sleep, movement, stress and meals first.

At GrowMedica.cz we work with a catalogue of biomedical supplements and related products. We insist on fair language: a food supplement **is not a medicine**, does not replace a varied diet, and does not replace individual advice from a doctor or pharmacist. If you take medication, are pregnant, breastfeeding, plan to give a product to a child, or live with a chronic condition, talk to a professional before you buy. The information below is educational.

## Why people buy supplements and what they are really solving

The most common reasons are ordinary: fatigue, seasonal changes, sport, a wish to “top something up”, skin and joint care, or the feeling that the diet is incomplete. Marketing often promises a fast change. Reality is slower. A supplement may help **fill gaps** in intake — but it will not erase chronic sleep debt, long-term stress or a sedentary routine.

When choosing, split your goal into three layers. Layer one is **the base**: sleep, hydration, protein, vegetables, movement. Layer two is a **targeted supplement** (for example vitamin D in winter with low sun exposure, iron only after a confirmed deficiency, omega-3 if fish intake is low). Layer three is **specialities** — herbs, mushroom extracts, complex formulas. The higher the layer, the more carefully you should read the label and the less you should trust shortcuts such as “natural = completely safe for everyone”.

In an online shop it helps to think in categories, not emotions. Instead of “I want to be fit tomorrow”, say “I want to compare vitamin C forms” or “I want collagen powder with a clear dose”. That reduces impulse buys and increases the chance the product fits your routine and budget.

Online catalogues offer filters and depth, but also risk of shortened copy. A good product page shows composition, pack size, cost per day and availability. If a page promises miracles and hides the ingredient list, leave — even if the price looks attractive.

### How to read a label without getting lost in marketing

A label is a contract between the manufacturer and you. Look for: recommended daily dose, amount of active ingredient in mg/µg, format (capsule, tablet, powder, syrup), excipients, warnings, and manufacturer/importer contact. A huge promise with tiny composition details is a red flag. Serious products can say “what” and “how much” without shouting.

For herbs and extracts, check plant part and type (powder, extract, standardised extract). “Herbal” does not mean free of drug interactions. For children and older adults, dosing is more sensitive. For syrups, check age suitability and sugars or sweeteners if that matters to you.

Also notice what a product **does not** contain when you have a reason to care: lactose, gluten, gelatine. Missing an ingredient does not automatically equal quality — you still need a clear dose and a meaningful goal.

**When a supplement makes sense — and when a pause is better.** It makes most sense with a clear reason: low intake of a nutrient, a specific life stage, professional advice, or a transparent goal (for example protein powder with higher training volume). If you buy “something against everything”, you end up with half-empty jars and the feeling that “supplements never work” — even though you never had a plan.

A pause is wise when you do not know what you already take, when you are in treatment and want to avoid interactions, or when you have not fixed the basics yet. A shop is a selection tool, not a substitute for medical assessment. With long fatigue, unexplained symptoms, strong pain or suspected disease, see a doctor first. A supplement may come later as support, not as the first and only answer.

A practical compromise for most people: one well-chosen product for 8–12 weeks, logged use, then evaluate. If after twelve weeks the routine itself has no meaning (not necessarily a “miracle”), stop and redefine the goal instead of auto-renewing.

## How to build a personal choice: from goal to product

A technically good choice is not the longest ingredient list on the front of the pack. It is the match between your goal, a format you will actually use, and a monthly budget. Start with one sentence: “I want to add vitamin C in winter” is better than “I want super immunity”. Then narrow the format and compare two or three items in the same class — not twenty different stories.

Cost per day helps. A more expensive pack can be cheaper per dose. A cheap pack with a dose you do not need is waste. Count daily tablets: if you must take six capsules and you know you will not, a simpler formula wins even with less exotic marketing.

In the GrowMedica catalogue it helps to walk the category tree instead of endless scrolling. The broad supplements category is a start; leaf categories (vitamins, collagen, herbs, healthy foods) reduce noise. Hierarchy is for orientation — not a reason to buy everything in a branch.

### A practical four-step process

**Step 1 — Goal.** One sentence measurable as a habit, not as a 48-hour result promise.  
**Step 2 — Product class.** Vitamin, mineral, protein, herb, food, topical — do not mix everything at once.  
**Step 3 — Format and dose.** What you can take every day without friction.  
**Step 4 — Label check.** Allergens, medicines, age, pregnancy, children, upper limits.

If you still hesitate between two products, pick the clearer composition and the more reasonable daily cost. Marketing completeness is not quality.

### Common mistakes that make shopping expensive

First mistake: stacking similar products (three “immune” complexes at once). Second: ignoring food and an existing multivitamin. Third: buying from influencers without context. Fourth: trying to “fix” a serious health problem only via an online shop. Fifth: poor storage and expiry. Sixth: chasing the highest number on the pack — more milligrams is not always better.

If you already own several packs, inventory before buying more. Write the name, dose and why you bought it. After a month you will see whether you even opened it. That feedback is often worth more than another “new hit”.

## Safety, YMYL and fair brand communication

Health content is YMYL (Your Money or Your Life). That means higher responsibility: no blog diagnoses, no treatment promises, no replacing medical care. A fair brand says what a product is, what it is not, and when to see a professional. Customers should leave knowing how to compare — not feeling they failed if they do not buy.

For herbs, tinctures and specialised formulas, risk is higher than for ordinary vitamin C. Drug interactions are real topics for pharmacists, not comment sections. If you take anticoagulants, thyroid medication, blood-pressure or mood medicines, or you have surgery planned, do not experiment blindly. The same applies in pregnancy, breastfeeding and children: doses and suitable forms are not universal.

#### How to recognise a serious product description

A serious description lists composition, recommended dose, who the product is **not** for, and does not rely on unverifiable before/after photos as proof. It may mention general nutrition context, but it does not say “cures”. If you need treatment for a disease, look for a doctor — not a blog or a product card alone.

#### How to recognise problematic marketing

Problematic marketing uses absolute words (always, never, 100%), fear urgency, unverifiable badges and shortcuts like “one capsule instead of all of medicine”. It pushes emotion faster than facts. A good offer survives your questions; a bad offer needs haste.

#### What to take away before you click “add to cart”

Check five points: (1) a one-sentence goal, (2) a dose and format you can keep, (3) cost per day, (4) label warnings, (5) conflicts with medicines, pregnancy or a child’s age. The cart can wait. Sound judgement is cheaper than returns and disappointment.

Finally: **the best supplement is the one whose composition you understand, whose dose you can keep, and which you do not need to defend with a miracle story.** Food supplements have a place in modern life — when you choose them as carefully as other decisions about money and health. Keep a one-page note (goal, medicines, allergies, budget, what you already own) and shopping becomes faster and fairer.
